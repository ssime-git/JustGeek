CHAPTER 06

1980 – 1987 · The Expert System Boom

Expert Systems and
the Commercial Compromise

After a decade of winter, AI came back. Not by solving the hard problem — but by lowering the ambition and sharpening the use case.

THE ARCHITECT OF THE APPROACH
Stanford · "Father of Expert Systems"
Edward Feigenbaum
Led DENDRAL (1965) — the first program to match a human specialist's performance. Then MYCIN (1972), which diagnosed bacterial infections as well as expert physicians. His insight: the bottleneck was not computing power but knowledge encoding. He named the discipline "knowledge engineering."
THE STRATEGIC PIVOT
Before (1956 – 1974)
General Intelligence
Build a machine that can think about anything. Massive ambition. Hit the same walls: not enough compute, not enough data, too much hype.
→
After (1980s)
Narrow Expertise
Build a machine that can do one valuable job extremely well. Encode human domain knowledge as if-then rules. Useful enough to sell.

XCON configured VAX computer orders for Digital Equipment Corporation — a task that previously required teams of specialized engineers. It could validate orders, suggest components, and ensure compatibility across DEC's product line. No learning. No adaptation. Just 2,500+ rules written by hand.
XCON RULE SAMPLE — VAX CONFIGURATION (simplified)
R-001 IF order contains VAX-11/780 THEN REQUIRE KD11-Z power supply
R-002 IF memory > 512KB AND bus = UNIBUS THEN ADD MS11-P expansion board
R-003 IF drive is RP06 AND controller is undefined THEN ASSIGN RH780 controller
R-004 IF cabinet capacity exceeded THEN ADD expansion cabinet BA11-K
⋮ 2,496 more rules covering every DEC product combination

XCON worked brilliantly — within its domain. The market responded: by 1988, expert systems had grown to a $2B+ industry. "AI" became a business term.

But expert systems had their own ceiling. Each required painstaking manual knowledge encoding. They could not learn. They could not generalize. Every new domain meant starting from scratch. The same pattern was forming again.

A real technical advance. Wildly oversold, again. The same wall, again: brittle, narrow, unable to generalize.
The Second AI Winter (1987–1993) followed on schedule.
