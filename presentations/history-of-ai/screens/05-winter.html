CHAPTER 05

1969 – 1980 · The First AI Winter

Minsky's Critique
and the First Winter

For a decade, Minsky and Rosenblatt argued. Then in 1969, Minsky and his co-author Seymour Papert ended the debate — mathematically. A single proof collapsed an entire research program.

THE AUTHORS OF THE BLOW
MIT · Symbolic AI
Marvin Minsky
Co-author of Perceptrons (1969). Had always believed neural nets were a dead end.
MIT · Mathematics
Seymour Papert
Provided the rigorous mathematical proofs showing the structural limits of single-layer networks.
THE KILLING BLOW — THE XOR PROBLEM
A perceptron can only draw a straight line through its input space to separate categories. XOR requires a boundary that bends — impossible for a single-layer network. Minsky proved this was not a fixable bug. It was a structural limit. Without a way to train multi-layer networks (the fix wouldn't come until backprop in the 1980s), neural networks looked like a dead end.

That cycle would become a constant in AI history. When the promise outruns the machinery, winter follows.

THE FIX THAT FINALLY CAME — BACKPROPAGATION (1986)

Minsky proved that single-layer perceptrons couldn't solve XOR. He did not prove that multi-layer networks couldn't. The missing piece was an efficient way to train multiple layers at once. In 1986, David Rumelhart, Geoffrey Hinton, and Ronald Williams published backpropagation — an algorithm that flows error signals backwards through every layer, adjusting all weights in a single pass. The XOR problem was now trivially solvable. Neural networks revived.

1969 · The limit
Single-layer only
XOR impossible. No way to train deeper networks. Dead end declared.
→
1986 · Backprop
Multi-layer unlocked
Error flows backward. All layers adjust. Neural nets revive — but still need more data and compute to scale.
