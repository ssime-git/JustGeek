CHAPTER 01

1939–1945 · Bletchley Park, England

Turing, Enigma,
and the First
Machine Intelligence

In 1939, Germany's Enigma machines encrypted military orders with 158 quintillion possible settings. Alan Turing's breakthrough was to reframe an impossible mental problem as a mechanical search problem.

ALAN TURING · 1912 – 1954
TURING'S KEY REFRAMING — HOW HE SOLVED THE UNSOLVABLE
THE MACHINE — BOMBE (1939)
REBUILT BOMBE · BLETCHLEY PARK MUSEUM
Designed byAlan Turing
Engineered byHarold Keen / BTM
First deliveryMarch 1940
Rotating drums108 per machine
Wire inside each~10 miles
Weight~1 ton each
Total produced210 machines
Peak throughput4,000+ msg/day

Not a computer. A mechanical elimination engine — 108 rotating drums spinning in parallel, hunting for the logical contradictions that rule out millions of Enigma settings at a time.

WHO BUILT IT
Concept & Mathematical Framework
Alan Turing
Cambridge mathematician. Reframed the cryptanalytic problem as mechanical search. Defined which contradictions to look for, how to chain them, and how to reduce quintillions to hundreds. The mathematics and the insight were entirely his.
Engineering & Physical Construction
Harold Keen
Chief engineer at British Tabulating Machine Company (BTM). Translated Turing's abstract method into working hardware: drum mechanisms, relay logic, cabling, and the production line that delivered 210 machines by 1945.

LASTING LEGACY — THE TURING REFRAME (1950)
The old question (1950)
Can machines THINK?
Unanswerable. Philosophical. Leads nowhere practical.
Turing's new question
What behavior would COUNT as thinking?
Testable. Behavioral. Changed the field before the field had a name. The same reframe that cracked Enigma — shift from philosophy to measurable criteria.
"Stop asking whether machines can think. Ask what would count as evidence that they can."
Alan Turing · "Computing Machinery and Intelligence" (1950)
