CHAPTER 02

Summer 1956 · Dartmouth College, New Hampshire

The Moment AI
Became a Field

After Turing, research on intelligent machines existed in fragments — no shared name, no community, no momentum. A field without a name can't attract funding, students, or institutions. In the summer of 1956, four men changed that.

ARTIFICIAL
INTELLIGENCE
Dartmouth College · July – August 1956 · The name that created the field
THE FOUR ORGANIZERS
McCarthy
Coined "Artificial Intelligence"
Minsky
Symbolic AI · Logic & Rules
Shannon
Information Theory
Rochester
IBM · First General-Purpose Computer Architect
"Every aspect of learning or any other feature of intelligence can be so precisely described that a machine can be made to simulate it."
Dartmouth Workshop Proposal · 1956

It was an ambitious label, maybe deliberately so. But naming the field worked. Once the question existed — how do you build a machine that behaves intelligently? — the room immediately split over the answer.

THE CENTRAL QUESTION SPLIT THE ROOM
Camp A
Symbolic AI
  • Intelligence is reason
  • Top-down: start with rules
  • Explicit knowledge representation
  • If-then logic trees
  • Brittle but interpretable
Champion: Marvin Minsky
Camp B
Neural Networks
  • Intelligence is adaptation
  • Bottom-up: learn from examples
  • Pattern discovery in data
  • Weighted connections
  • Flexible but opaque
Champion: Frank Rosenblatt

The question split the room on day one — and neither side would concede for a decade. Both camps would go on to build real systems, generate real hype, and hit real walls.
